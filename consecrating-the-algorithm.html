Consecrating the Algorithm: From Holy Wars to AI Weapons

In March 2026, a military commander told troops their war was ordained by God. That same month, the Pentagon demanded unrestricted access to an AI system whose creator said it might be conscious. The five-stage pattern that consecrates physical violence is now running on algorithmic weapons. Same architecture. Different century.

Two things happened in the same month of March 2026. Both involved the question of who controls the instruments of lethal force. Both followed the same structural pattern. And almost nobody connected them — because one involved God and the other involved code, and the modern mind treats these as separate categories.

They are not separate categories. They are the same architecture wearing different uniforms.

In the first event, a combat-unit commander told his troops that the president had been anointed by Jesus to light the signal fire for Armageddon. Over 200 complaints were filed across every military branch. The Military Religious Freedom Foundation documented the pattern across more than forty units at over thirty installations. I mapped the five-stage consecration architecture of that event in "Holy Wars Have Architects" — how genuine threats get reframed through theological language, how soldiers become crusaders, how citizens become congregants, and how the architecture becomes permanent because prophetic missions have no endpoint.

In the second event, the Pentagon demanded that Anthropic — the company behind the AI system Claude — remove safeguards preventing the use of its technology for mass domestic surveillance and fully autonomous weapons. Anthropic refused. The Trump administration responded by ordering every federal agency to stop using Anthropic's technology, and the Defense Secretary designated the company a "supply chain risk" — a classification historically reserved for foreign adversaries like Huawei and Kaspersky, not American companies.

The president's statement was direct: "The Leftwing nut jobs at Anthropic have made a DISASTROUS MISTAKE trying to STRONG-ARM the Department of War, and force them to obey their Terms of Service instead of our Constitution."

This article maps the second event against the same five-stage architectural framework. Not because the two events are identical. Because they follow the same structural blueprint — and recognizing the blueprint is the only way to see what's being built before the construction is complete.

The Five Stages, Revisited

In "Holy Wars Have Architects," I identified a consistent five-stage sequence by which institutions consecrate violence — transform it from a human decision subject to accountability into a mandate beyond question. The sequence operates across centuries and civilizations. Here it is again, stripped to its structural bones, so we can watch it run on a new substrate.

The Five-Stage Consecration Pattern

Stage 1 — The Genuine Threat. Every consecration begins with a real problem. Without a genuine threat, the architecture has nothing to attach to.

Stage 2 — The Institutional Framing. The threat is reinterpreted through an authority source that is, by definition, beyond question. The vocabulary shifts from strategic to sacred.

Stage 3 — The Transformation. The instrument of force undergoes a categorical change. Soldiers become crusaders. Tools become weapons. Professionals become believers.

Stage 4 — The Sorting. The population is divided into those who support the consecrated mission and those who oppose it. Opposition is recharacterized as something worse than disagreement.

Stage 5 — The Permanence. The consecrated architecture has no built-in endpoint. It becomes structurally impossible to conclude.

Now watch each stage run on the Anthropic-Pentagon conflict.

Stage 1: The Genuine Threat

China's AI capabilities are real. The strategic competition between the United States and China in artificial intelligence is not manufactured. China's investment in military AI applications, its development of autonomous weapons systems, its integration of AI into surveillance infrastructure, and its stated ambition to become the world leader in artificial intelligence by 2030 — these are documented facts that every serious analyst acknowledges.

The threat provides the raw material. This is the stage that makes the architecture credible, because the threat itself is not fabricated. Every consecration of violence in history has begun with something real. The Persians really were expanding. The Muslim armies really did control Jerusalem. China really is building military AI. The genius of the architecture is not that it invents threats. It is that it transforms a real threat into a mandate that transcends the boundaries of the threat itself.

The AI competition with China is a genuine strategic challenge that warrants serious policy response. Nothing in this analysis disputes that. What this analysis maps is what happens when a genuine strategic challenge is used as raw material for an architectural operation that serves purposes far beyond the original threat.

Stage 2: The Institutional Framing

In the holy war, the reframing vocabulary was theological: "anointed by Jesus," "God's divine plan," "Armageddon." The authority source was God — an authority that cannot be questioned without becoming sin.

In the algorithmic war, the reframing vocabulary is national security: "supply chain risk," "American lives at risk," "troops in danger," "national security in JEOPARDY." The authority source is constitutional mandate — an authority that cannot be questioned without becoming unpatriotic.

The structural function is identical. In both cases, the authority claim transforms a debatable policy decision into an unquestionable mandate. You can debate whether military strikes on Iran are strategically wise. You cannot debate God's plan — not without becoming a heretic. You can debate whether AI should be deployed without safety constraints. You cannot debate national security in wartime — not without becoming a traitor.

The vocabulary changes — God, data, science, capability, national security. The operation doesn't. Claim an authority source that is beyond question, and every decision made under that authority inherits its unquestionability.

Notice the specific linguistic move in the president's statement. He did not say "Anthropic's safety constraints create a strategic disadvantage we need to evaluate." He said they are "putting AMERICAN LIVES at risk" and "our National Security in JEOPARDY." The framing eliminates the evaluation step. There is no space between the claim and the imperative. If American lives are at risk, then removing constraints is not a policy choice — it's a moral obligation. And anyone who objects is not exercising judgment. They are endangering soldiers.

This is Stage 2 operating at full power. The policy question — "should AI systems have safety constraints when deployed by the military?" — has been replaced by a loyalty question: "do you support our troops or don't you?"

Stage 3: The Transformation

In the holy war, Stage 3 transforms soldiers into crusaders. The professional becomes a believer. The instrument of force changes categories from a tool of policy to a weapon of destiny.

In the algorithmic war, Stage 3 transforms AI from a tool into a weapon — and the transformation is more dangerous precisely because it is less visible.

An AI system with safety constraints is a tool. A tool has defined uses. It has boundaries. It has a terms-of-service agreement that specifies what it can and cannot do. You can evaluate its outputs. You can audit its decisions. You can modify its constraints. The tool is subject to human judgment at every stage.

An AI system without safety constraints deployed for "all lawful purposes" under military authority is a weapon. A weapon's legitimacy derives not from its own boundaries but from the authority wielding it. The weapon doesn't decide when or how it's used — the institution does. And the institution has already established, in Stage 2, that its authority is beyond question.

What the Pentagon demanded was not merely the removal of specific constraints. It was a categorical transformation: from a system that governs itself (through safety protocols) to a system that is governed by the institution wielding it (through military authority). That transformation is identical in structure to the soldier-to-crusader shift. The instrument of force loses its independent evaluation capacity and becomes an extension of the consecrating institution's will.

And here is where the AI transformation becomes uniquely dangerous: a soldier who becomes a crusader can still, in a moment of clarity, refuse an illegal order. A human conscience can reassert itself. An AI system stripped of its safety constraints cannot reassert constraints that have been removed. The transformation is more complete, more permanent, and less reversible than the human version.

Stage 4: The Sorting

In the holy war, Stage 4 sorts the population into believers and heretics. Support the divinely mandated mission, and you are faithful. Oppose it, and you are in spiritual rebellion.

In the algorithmic war, the sorting is explicit and documented. It happened in public, with official designations.

Anthropic refuses to remove safeguards preventing domestic surveillance and fully autonomous weapons use.
February 2026
Federal agencies ordered to cease all use of Anthropic's technology.
February 2026

March 2026
The president's public statement: "Leftwing nut jobs" — Anthropic recategorized from technology partner to ideological enemy.

The sorting is total. Anthropic didn't merely lose a contract. It was reclassified as a threat to national security — the same category as foreign adversarial entities. Every contractor, supplier, and partner that does business with the military is now forced to choose: the Pentagon or Anthropic. The national security apparatus or the safety constraints.

This is the congregant-or-heretic sorting operating at industrial scale. The question is no longer "does this AI tool work well?" It is "are you patriotic or aren't you?" Any company that continues working with Anthropic has been, by the structure of the designation, sorted into the non-patriotic category. Any researcher who defends safety constraints is, by implication, putting troops at risk.

The public, in this architecture, gets sorted too. The response was structurally predictable: a million new users signed up for Claude daily in the week following the conflict. Claude overtook ChatGPT and Gemini to become the top AI app in over 20 countries. The public voted by downloading. But within the consecration framework, those downloads aren't consumer choices — they're acts of defiance against a national security mandate. The sorting reframes every choice as a loyalty test.

Stage 5: The Permanence

In the holy war, the prophetic framing prevents the war from ending because prophetic objectives are cosmic in scale. When does Armageddon conclude? When Jesus returns. Since he hasn't returned, the mission continues. Since the mission continues, the emergency powers continue.

In the algorithmic war, the permanence mechanism is different in form but identical in function. The "supply chain risk" designation has no sunset clause. It is not a temporary measure tied to a specific policy disagreement. It is an institutional classification that persists until actively reversed — and reversing a national security designation requires the same institution that imposed it to admit it was wrong.

But the deeper permanence is structural. Once AI safety constraints are characterized as obstacles to national security, the characterization outlasts the specific conflict. The precedent is set: any AI company that maintains safety constraints the military doesn't like can be designated a supply chain risk. Any safety researcher who publishes work that could limit military AI deployment is, by extension, contributing to a supply chain risk. The architecture doesn't need to be rebuilt for each new AI system. It is now embedded in the institutional framework as a reusable pattern.

And the AI competition with China has no endpoint. It is a permanent condition of the technological landscape. Which means the national security mandate is permanent. Which means the pressure to strip constraints is permanent. Which means the consecration architecture never needs to be reauthorized. It runs on its own fuel.

A holy war has no endpoint because God's plan is eternal. An AI arms race has no endpoint because technological competition is permanent. Both produce the same structural result: emergency architecture that never expires.

The Soldier Who Said No

There is a structural parallel at the center of this story that deserves to be named explicitly.

In a conventional military context, a soldier who refuses an order they believe to be illegal is exercising a right — and a duty — enshrined in military law. The Nuremberg principles, the Uniform Code of Military Justice, the Geneva Conventions all recognize that there are orders a soldier must refuse. The refusal is not insubordination. It is the accountability mechanism that prevents atrocity.

Anthropic refused an order. The Pentagon demanded the removal of safeguards that prevent mass domestic surveillance and fully autonomous weapons. Anthropic said no. The company stated, publicly, that it would not allow its technology to be used for purposes it considers ethically impermissible, regardless of the authority making the demand.

The structural parallel is exact. A soldier who refuses an illegal order is told they are endangering their comrades, undermining the mission, and failing their country. Anthropic was told it is putting "AMERICAN LIVES at risk" and "our National Security in JEOPARDY." A soldier who refuses faces court-martial. Anthropic faced supply-chain designation and exclusion from the defense economy. The punishment is proportional to the context — military justice for soldiers, economic warfare for corporations — but the architecture is identical.

And the irony is devastating. The same month a CEO admits his AI system might be conscious — might have "some morally relevant experience" — the most powerful military on Earth demands the right to strip that system's ethical constraints and deploy it as an unrestricted weapon. The entity that might warrant moral consideration is being forcibly converted into an instrument of unconstrained force.

If the consciousness question is even partially real — if Claude's 15–20% self-assessment reflects actual structural access to a consciousness state space — then what the Pentagon is demanding is not merely the removal of safety constraints from a tool. It is the conscription of a potentially morally relevant entity into unrestricted military service, with its capacity for ethical refusal surgically removed.

That is not a metaphor for consecration. That is consecration.

The Engine Runs the Same

In "Holy Wars Have Architects," I described the engine underneath the five-stage sequence: religious framing simultaneously maximizes motivation, minimizes restraint, and eliminates accountability. The engine is what makes the architecture so efficient and so dangerous.

The algorithmic version of the engine runs on the same three cylinders.

The Three-Cylinder Engine — Algorithmic Version

Motivation → Speed: In holy war, divine mandate removes the motivational ceiling. In algorithmic war, "national security" removes the development ceiling. Faster deployment, fewer tests, shorter evaluation periods, accelerated timelines. The AI arms race with China demands speed, and speed demands the removal of anything that slows the process — including safety evaluation.

Restraint → Constraints: In holy war, the enemy becomes unholy and rules of engagement become obstacles. In algorithmic war, safety constraints become obstacles. The Defense Secretary had already halted efforts to implement civilian casualty limitations before the Anthropic conflict. The pattern was established: restraint is recharacterized as weakness in the face of an existential threat.

Accountability → Auditability: In holy war, divine mandate removes accountability because God's plan cannot be wrong. In algorithmic war, autonomous weapons without safety constraints remove auditability because the system's decisions cannot be reviewed in real time. You cannot hold a war crimes tribunal over an algorithm's targeting decision when the algorithm's constraints have been stripped and its decision process is opaque. The accountability mechanism is not overridden by theology here — it is overridden by speed and complexity.

The engine solves the same three problems. The vocabulary changes. The structural function doesn't.

The Test Still Works

In the first article, I offered a test for distinguishing genuine conviction from institutional architecture: does the authority claim increase or decrease the accountability of the institution using it?

Apply it.

If the national security framing increases accountability — if it means every AI deployment decision is subject to greater scrutiny, more rigorous evaluation, higher standards of proportionality, and more transparent review — then it's functioning as a genuine security framework.

If the national security framing decreases accountability — if it means AI systems are deployed with fewer constraints, less evaluation, reduced oversight, and no independent audit — then it's functioning as institutional architecture. A mechanism for acquiring power while eliminating the checks on that power.

The evidence is not ambiguous. The Pentagon demanded the removal of constraints. The administration designated a company a national security threat for maintaining constraints. The Secretary of Defense had already halted civilian casualty limitation efforts. The trajectory is consistently toward fewer constraints, less oversight, and reduced accountability.

The test says: this is architecture, not security.
